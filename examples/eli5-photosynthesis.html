Photosynthesis

Plants eat sunlight. They take in light, water, and air, and turn it into food (sugar) for themselves — and oxygen for us as a free bonus.

💡 The Analogy

Imagine a tiny factory inside every leaf. This factory has solar panels on the roof (the green parts called chlorophyll) that capture energy from the sun. Through pipes in the walls, the factory receives two raw materials: water pumped up from the roots through the stem, and carbon dioxide — a gas that drifts in through tiny pores on the leaf's surface (like little breathing holes called stomata).

Inside the factory, the solar energy powers a machine that rips apart the water molecules and rearranges the atoms from water and CO₂ into something new: sugar — the plant's food. It's like building LEGO: you take apart one set (water + CO₂) and snap the pieces together into a completely different creation (sugar). The leftover oxygen atoms? They get pushed out through those same breathing holes — which is the air we breathe.

Where this analogy breaks down

A real factory runs on electricity from a power grid. A leaf's "factory" generates its own power from sunlight in real-time — it can't store electricity for later the way a battery can. Also, the "rearranging atoms" step isn't one simple machine — it's a chain of dozens of chemical reactions happening in sequence, more like an assembly line than a single device.

📖 A Bit More Detail

Why are plants green?

The "solar panels" in the factory analogy are molecules of chlorophyll — a pigment that absorbs red and blue light but bounces green light back to your eyes. That's it — plants are green because green is the color of sunlight they don't use. They're actually using the rest of the rainbow.

The two-shift factory

Photosynthesis actually runs in two stages — think of it as a day shift and a second shift. The day shift (light-dependent reactions) can only happen when the sun is shining. It splits water, captures the energy, and produces oxygen as a byproduct. The second shift (the Calvin cycle) takes the energy captured by the day shift and uses it to assemble CO₂ into sugar. Despite its name, the second shift can technically run without light — it just needs the energy the day shift produced.

Why this matters for all of us

Almost all food on Earth traces back to photosynthesis. Plants make sugar from sunlight. Animals eat plants. Other animals eat those animals. The energy flowing through every food chain started as sunlight hitting a leaf. Meanwhile, the oxygen we breathe is literally a waste product of this process — plants don't "want" to make oxygen, it's just leftover hydrogen from the water molecules they cracked open.

The part that trips people up

Most people think plants "breathe in" CO₂ and "breathe out" O₂ — as if they're the opposite of animals. The truth is plants do BOTH. They photosynthesize (using CO₂, releasing O₂) AND they respire (using O₂, releasing CO₂) — just like we do. During the day, photosynthesis produces more O₂ than respiration uses, so the net effect is oxygen out. At night, with no sunlight, they only respire — they're quietly consuming oxygen just like you are.
